The Cave Allegory in
Science Fiction Cinema

Man has gone out to explore other worlds and other civilizations without having explored his own labyrinth of dark passages and secret chambers, and without finding what lies behind doorways that he himself has sealed.

—Stanisław Lem, Solaris (1961)

The Cave Allegory

And now allow me to draw a comparison in order to understand the effect of learning (or the lack thereof) upon our nature. Imagine that there are people living in a cave deep underground. ... Then, undoubtedly, such captives would consider the truth to be nothing but the shadows of the carved objects.

—Plato, The Republic 514a–515c

Plato’s Allegory of the Cave in The Republic Book VII (375 B.C.) remains one of the most profound and enduring metaphors for knowledge, enlightenment and the nature of reality. In this allegory, Plato describes a scenario where prisoners are confined to a dark cave since birth and chained in such a way that they can only see an inner wall. As each day passes, shadows are cast upon this wall by objects and moving people behind them. Mistaking these shadows for reality, the prisoners accept them as the only truth they know.

One day, a prisoner breaks free from the chains and discover the true sources of the shadows. In doing so, this freed prisoner abandons their old, limited perspective. And when they return to the cave, they recognise the shadows as mere reflections of the broader world, to the dismay of the fellow prisoners who violently reject the new findings.

Although Plato originally used the Cave Allegory to describe his personal struggle to educate an ignorant and hostile Athenian public, it is equally a powerful metaphor for scientific advancement. The chained prisoners represent the general public who experience surface appearances of the world and are confined to partial truths shaped by human limitations. For example, people observe apples falling from trees without comprehending the forces invisible to the human eye: like gravity. The prisoner who breaks free are like scientists who attempt to reason and understand deeper truths that lie beyond the ordinary human perspective.

The Cave Allegory in Sci-Fi Cinema

Plato’s Cave Allegory has made many appearances in 20th and 21st century science fiction cinema. The Cave Allegory is often used in these films to explore how science’s complex relationship with humanity.

In this feature article, we will examine how each of the three science fiction films: 2001: A Space Odyssey (1968), Solaris (1972) and Arrival (2016) either support or challenge the position of science as the liberating force in Plato’s Cave Allegory.

2001: A Space Odyssey

Stanley Kubrick’s 2001: A Space Odyssey presents science as the key for humanity to escape Plato’s cave of ignorance and limitation. The film’s opening Dawn of Man sequence acts as a parable of Plato’s Cave Allegory, set in a prehistoric age when the first humans emerged. In this scene (shown in figure 7), Kubrick depicts the proto humans huddled fearfully inside a dark cave, an obvious allusion to the Platonic allegory. Like Plato’s prisoners, these early humans are bound by fear and ignorance, powerless before nature and rival tribes. However, after encountering an alien monolith, a higher order being or artefact, the proto humans discover how to wield a bone as a weapon which they use to defeat another tribe.

In a moment of triumph, the proto human flings the bone into the air, and Kubrick match cuts to a spacecraft drifting in orbit (figure 6). Although the time between the two cuts is separated by millions of years, in completely different environments, the bone and the spacecraft match graphically. Through this iconic transition, Kubrick connects the bone (a prehistoric invention) to modern scientific advancements that have replaced it yet serve the same purpose: helping humans to navigate and explore the universe. In this sense, the cave of Plato’s allegory has expanded outward. Earth itself becomes the cave, while the vast cosmos represents the world outside.

Evidently, 2001 presents science as the means for humanity to escape our Earth-bound condition. The film’s spaceships symbolise the power of science not only as a means of transporting David Bowman to Jupiter but also to the Monolith. In doing so, Kubrick describes the two-way relationship between mankind and science.

Humankind can advance science as seen in the aforementioned match cut, but science can in turn elevate humanity to more enlightened state. In this way, science becomes the vehicle that allows Plato’s prisoners to escape from their chained existence and search for greater meaning and knowledge beyond.

However, 2001 also subtly warns us about the misuse of science. Kubrick suggests that science when channelled through technology, is often used to serve selfish human interests rather than to uncover the deeper and greater truths about the universe.

The misuse of science is captured by the famous shot where the pen glides wistfully in mid-air (figure 8). Kubrick uses the shallow depth of field and central framing to draw the audience’s focus to the pen: a symbol of humanity’s pursuit of knowledge, reason and enlightenment. Yet the pen is unmoored from the passenger’s hand who is asleep in the background, as the video screen flickers in the background. The dramatic irony between the pen’s clear prominence and the passenger’s ignorance of it suggests that science can be applied to satisfy human comfort and complacency rather than to advance our understanding.

Kubrick also suggests sciences’ role in warfare. Returning to the iconic cut from the bone to the spacecraft, in the Arthur C. Clarke novel, written alongside the film, suggests that though science has advanced, human aggression has remained unchanged. “In a million years, the human race had lost few of its aggressive instincts; along symbolic lines visible only to politicians, the thirty-eight nuclear powers watched one another with belligerent anxiety.” This detail, though unstated in the film, shows how science is ultimately used to serve human aggression rather than a means for enlightenment.

Kubrick is clearly criticising science’s misuse during the 1960 Cold War era, which saw the proliferation of atomic weapons. The Atomic Bomb itself shows how physics, a scientific discipline intended to uncover the laws of the universe was redirected to impose domination and fear. His criticism extends to consumer culture of the 1960s, where post-war scientific developments resulted in an explosion of household appliances such as television, microwaves and dishwashers (G. Cross 2000). Kubrick’s shot of the floating pen suggests that these inventions do little to advance our collective understanding.

Solaris

Andrei Tarkovsky’s Solaris (1972) is a direct response to Kubrick’s 2001: A Space Odyssey. Solaris follows psychologist Kris Kelvin who travels to a space station orbiting the mysterious planet called Solaris. As the scientists attempt to uncover Solaris’ mystery, the planet’s ocean confronts them with physical manifestations of their memories.

Tarkovsky’s Solaris offers a scathing critique of 2001’s view that proposes science as the path to transcendence. Whereas Kubrick’s film looks outward, suggesting that enlightenment lies amongst the stars, Tarkovsky turns inward. True enlightenment, Tarkovsky suggests, can only be achieved through deep self-reflection and not through scientific progress.

Tarkovsky begins his critique by attacking the centrepiece of 2001’s thesis: Kubrick’s iconic match cut. Whereas 2001’s match cut leaps instantly from prehistory to the space age, Kris Kelvin’s journey to the Solaris space station is drawn out (figure 10). His trip to the space station involves a 5-minute bus ride through congested city traffic. Tarkovsky’s visual metaphor argues that scientific advancement is not a clear, linear leap forward, but a slow, messy process filled with obstacles and danger. This criticism is true as science is never straightforward, but instead depends on guesswork, experimentation, and constant revision. Thomas Kuhn, a historian and philosopher, argues in The Structure of Scientific Revolutions (1962) that scientific breakthroughs are “the tradition-shattering complements to the tradition-bound activity of normal science.” Newtonian physics, for example, was later challenged by Einstein’s theory of relativity.

This criticism is furthered by the films’ depiction of the spaceship, which runs counterpoint to the spacecraft in 2001. Kubrick’s choice to use the Blue Danube waltz (figure 11) evokes the Romantic Sublime, by turning space travel into a graceful dance amongst the stars. Meanwhile, in Solaris, the spaceship is depicted as cold, sterile, and hostile to human life, as shown in Kris Kelvin’s room onboard the space station (figure 12). Moreover, the scientific research conducted on the space station yields no results. The planet’s mysterious abilities remain unsolved. Through this, Solaris criticises the space race between the Soviet Union and the United States, suggesting that both superpowers are pouring vast resources into exploring an inhospitable void.

In Solaris, the Platonic higher being beyond the cave takes the form of the planet itself, subverting usual depictions of extraterrestrial beings. However, any attempt at communication with this higher intelligence proves impossible. The planet resurrects Khari (figure 13), Kris Kelvin’s former lover who died tragically. Yet she is recreated only from Kelvin’s own flawed and incomplete memories. Through this, Tarkovsky suggests that even when faced with a higher reality, humans remain bound by their own limitations. Our flawed memories cannot perfectly replicate reality, which prevents true understanding or meaningful connection with the higher beings like the monolith or Solaris. Khari remains a shadow, an imperfect copy that cannot bridge the gap between Solaris and Kris Kelvin.

Clearly, Tarkovsky is arguing that science is not the liberating force in the Platonic cave allegory. He portrays science as messy and incomplete, often only a half-truth, and condemns space travel as inhospitable. Furthermore, scientific advancement in space travel cannot lead us to higher beings because our flawed human perspective cannot grasp the greater truths embodied by the ‘higher beings.’ In fact, Tarkovsky suggests that the state of ignorance, like the prisoners inside the cave, is not completely undesirable. When asked about the mysteries of happiness, death and love in the final act, Kelvin responds: “Not knowing this day [of your death] is what makes us immortal.”

Tarkovsky’s message resonates with modern audiences. The image taken from the Voyager I spaceship dubbed the ‘Pale Blue Dot’ (figure 14) has confronted us with humanity’s insignificance in the vast, empty universe (NASA 2025). Perhaps, Tarkovsky suggests, it is be better to be ignorant of these overwhelming truths to preserve our fragile sense of wonder, hope and meaning.

Arrival

Denis Villeneuve’s Arrival also engages with Plato’s Cave Allegory but presents linguistics (a social science) as the core vessel transporting humanity from the cave to the greater world. In the film, humanity is contacted by mysterious alien beings called Heptapods. Like the planet Solaris, the film provides a unique depiction of alien life, as the Heptapods speak in language derived from their non-linear perception of time.

Villeneuve suggests that hard sciences like Mathematics and Physics alone cannot fill the divide between humanity and these “higher beings.” This is represented through the Ian Donnelly character, a physicist who fails to decode the alien’s mysterious language. Instead, Villeneuve foregrounds the Sapir-Whorf Hypothesis, a theory that proposes that language shapes thought. In the end, it is Dr. Louise Banks, a professor of linguistics that decodes the alien’s language, and by understanding their language, able to shape her own thought.

The human limitation of linear time perception is illustrated by the film’s subversion of the beleaguered mother trope. Throughout the film, we are shown memories of Banks’ dead child (figure 17). We as the audience assume these memories to be flashbacks because of our own linear understanding of time. Later, it is revealed that these flashbacks are actually flash forwards in time, depicting her future child with Ian that will tragically die young. Despite the Heptapods warning her about her bleak future, Banks still embraces his life with Ian. This is reflected by her name for her daughter, “Hannah” which is a palindrome, suggesting the non-linear perception of time that Banks now inhabits.

This films central misunderstanding demonstrates the shift from the prisoners of the cave to the enlightened thinkers. The Heptapods’ offer to share their language is misinterpreted as “Offer weapon” causing fear among world leaders much like Plato’s prisoners who are initially hostile to the returning prisoner. The Chinese commander, General Shang breaks negotiations and then attacks the alien vessel. Yet ironically it is the alien’s language that becomes the means of reconciliation. Banks, armed with ability to see forward in time is able to convince Shang to call off the attack by relaying to him the last words of his late wife. In this way, Banks, like the freed prisoner returning to the cave, uses her expanded vision to guide the prisoners to a greater truth. The transformative power of linguistics is summarised by General Shang’s final confession to Banks (figure 17): “You changed my mind. In a way, you are the reason for the unification.”

Villeneuve’s Arrival is thus aligned to Kubrick’s 2001. Science can transform humanity into higher states of being by facilitating contact with advanced alien beings. However, unlike 2001 which looks outward to the frontiers of space travel, Villeneuve argues that it is the science of language that fosters understanding with these higher beings, and is ultimately responsible for enlightenment.

Conclusion

Plato’s Cave Allegory continues to resonate with modern audiences because it captures the timeless struggle for truth in a vast, uncaring universe. 2001: A Space Odyssey presents science as the tool for truth seeking that can elevate humanity. Solaris challenges this view, revealing the limits of scientific understanding and the subjectivity of the human experience. Arrival offers a middle ground, suggesting that true enlightenment may come not just from scientific developments in space travel, but from the scientific study of languages and communication.

While it is unclear which film offers the ‘correct’ stance on science and its role in our search for truth, one thing is for certain: science will continue to be questioned, challenged and examined by cinema for generations to come.
