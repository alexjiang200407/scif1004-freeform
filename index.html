The Cave Allegory

And now allow me to draw a comparison in order to understand the effect of learning (or the lack thereof) upon our nature. Imagine that there are people living in a cave deep underground. ... Then, undoubtedly, such captives would consider the truth to be nothing but the shadows of the carved objects.

—Plato, The Republic 514a–515c

Plato’s Allegory of the Cave in The Republic Book VII (375 B.C.) remains one of the most profound and enduring metaphors for knowledge, enlightenment and the nature of reality. In this allegory, Plato presents a scenario where prisoners are confined to a dark cave since birth and chained in such a way that they can only see an inner wall. As each day passes, shadows are cast upon this wall by objects and moving people behind them. Mistaking these shadows for reality, the prisoners accept them as the only truth they know. One day, a prisoner breaks free from the chains and discover the true sources of the shadows. In doing so, this freed prisoner abandons their old, limited perspective. And when they return to the cave, they recognise the shadows as mere reflections of the broader world, to the dismay of the fellow prisoners who violently reject his new findings.

Although Plato originally used the Cave Allegory to describe his personal struggle to educate an ignorant and hostile Athenian public, it is equally a powerful metaphor describing scientific advancement. The chained prisoners represent the general public who experience surface appearances of the world and are confined to partial truths shaped by human limitations. For example, people observe apples falling from trees without comprehending the invisible forces like gravity. The prisoner who breaks free are like scientists who attempt to reason and understand deeper truths that lie beyond the ordinary human perspective.

The Cave Allegory in Sci-Fi Cinema

It is then no accident that Plato’s conclusions in Cave Allegory has been challenged and reaffirmed in science fiction films throughout the 20th and 21st centuries.

In this feature article, we will discuss two of the most influential sci-fi films of all time: Solaris (1972) and 2001 A Space Odyssey (1968). Both films grapple with the ideas presented by Plato’s Cave allegory but take opposite perspectives. While 2001 suggests that science and technology are essential steps for humanity to break its chains and reach higher truths, Solaris warns that even when we escape the cave, we may find that our own limitations and doubts elude us.

2001: A Space Odyssey

Stanley Kubrick’s 2001: A Space Odyssey (1968) presents a complex and balanced perspective on science and its relationship with humanity. Kubrick suggests that while science itself may not be the ultimate form of truth it remains a vital tool to propel humanity towards greater knowledge and higher states of being.

Kubrick draws on Plato’s Cave Allegory as a metaphor for humanity’s collective evolution. This idea is vividly illustrated in the film’s opening Dawn of Man sequence which depicts a pre-historic age supposedly when the first humans arose. In this scene, Kubrick depicts the proto humans huddled fearfully inside a cave, an obvious allusion to the Platonic prisoners of the cave. Like Plato’s prisoners, these early humans are bound by fear and ignorance, powerless before nature and rival tribes. However, after encountering an alien monolith, a symbol of the greater world outside the cave, the proto humans discover how to wield a bone as a weapon which they use to defeat another tribe.

In a moment of triumph, the proto human flings the bone into the air, and Kubrick match cuts to a spacecraft drifting in orbit. Although the time between the two cuts is separated by millions of years, in completely different environments, the bone and the spacecraft match graphically. Through this iconic transition, Kubrick connects the bone (a prehistoric invention) to modern scientific advancements that have replaced it yet serve the same purpose: helping humans to navigate and explore the universe. In this sense, the cave of Plato’s allegory has expanded outward. Earth itself becomes the cave, while the vast cosmos represents the world outside.

As you can see. Although 2001 argues for leaving the cave, however it also criticises how humanity applies science through technology. Instead, he calls for humanity to use science as a vessel to uncover the deeper and greater truths of the universe.

Scientific advancement renders human truth seeking obsolete

2001 presents a world where scientific progress has far outpaced human development. Throughout the film, Kubrick highlights humanity’s insignificance compared to its own monumental scientific and technological creations, by visually dwarfing the astronauts against vast, imposing spacecraft. However, the humanity’s smallness isn’t just physical but also intellectual embodied by HAL9000 the spaceship’s artificial intelligence (AI).

HAL9000 is by far the most ‘human’ character in the movie possessing a fear of death, emotions, deception, complications and is the one forced to make ethical choices. This is contrast to the cold and machine-like human astronauts. Kubrick’s use of a fisheye lens in HAL’s point-of-view shots further emphasizes humanity’s intellectual smallness within its own technological domain.

Indeed, in 2001 it is machines that are striving for knowledge and understanding, while humanity remains passive operators of these tools. It is only when David Bowman the last surviving astronaut disconnects HAL’s brain that he is able to transcend and morph into a higher being.

Kubrick’s concerns are extremely relevant today, with the 21st century’s relentless push toward automation and artificial intelligence, products of scientific advancement. In THE RISKS OF HUMAN OVERRELIANCE ON LARGE LANGUAGE MODELS FOR CRITICAL THINKING, AI researchers Dueneus and Ruiz examine how excessive dependence on AI tools can undermine human cognition and critical thought. The paper argues that the overreliance on these tools can cause “cognitive atrophy” and “exacerbate existing inequalities and create new forms of cognitive divide.”
